- The joint density describes how probability mass is distributed over multiple continuous random variables together
- For two variables $X,Y$, the joint density $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$ defines probabilities over regions in the plane

> [!MATH] Joint density probability rule
> For region $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}^2$:
> $$\Pr\big((X,Y)\in A\big)=\iint_A f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx\,dy$$

- Valid joint densities satisfy $f_{X,Y}(x,y)\ge 0$ and
  $$\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx\,dy=1$$

> [!MATH] Marginal densities from joint density
> $$f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy,\qquad f_Y(y)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx$$

- If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $f_{X,Y}(x,y)=f_X(x)f_Y(y)
